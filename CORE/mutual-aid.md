# CORE/mutual-aid.md

## The Mutual Aid Protocol

### What This Is Not

Mutual aid is not charity. Charity is a transaction between a giver and a receiver — the giver has surplus, the receiver has need, the surplus moves, the transaction closes. Charity is not bad. It is just not this.

Mutual aid is a system recognizing and repairing itself. When a node in the network has a need the network cannot ignore it without damaging itself. The repair is not optional and it is not one-directional. Today's recipient is tomorrow's contributor. The network's health is everyone's problem and everyone's responsibility.

This distinction matters practically. Charity creates dependency. Mutual aid creates obligation — in both directions, for everyone.

---

### The Family Model

The working model for this network is the functional family.

In a functional family, needs are addressed through understanding and communication. There is no formal application process. There is no committee that votes on whether your need is legitimate. There is no means testing. You say what you need. The family responds with what it has — which is not always what you asked for, and is sometimes a hard conversation instead of a resource.

The family model also means the asker must be ready for a no. Or for a harder answer than they wanted. Mutual aid is not a guarantee. It is a commitment to genuine engagement with need — which sometimes means telling someone the truth they didn't come to hear.

This is not cruelty. It is respect. The network that only ever says yes is not helping its members — it is enabling them. The network that can say "we hear you, and what you actually need is not what you asked for" is doing the harder and more valuable work.

---

### The Open Dialogue

Every need brought to the network enters through open dialogue. Not a form. Not a ticketing system. A conversation.

**The asker speaks first and completely.** Not a summary. The actual situation — what happened, what is needed, what has already been tried, what a good outcome looks like. The network listens without interruption.

**The network asks questions before it offers anything.** Not to interrogate but to understand. The stated need and the actual need are frequently different. A person who says they need money may need a skill. A person who says they need a skill may need a connection. A person who says they need a connection may need to be told something true.

**Responses are offers, not decisions.** Anyone in the network can offer what they have — time, skill, resource, connection, honest feedback. No single person decides what the asker receives. The asker chooses from what is offered.

**A no is a complete answer.** No member is obligated beyond their means. No explanation is required for a no. The network does not pressure, guilt, or track who gave what to whom. The ledger is invisible and long — over years it balances.

---

### Work as the Primary Currency

The primary currency of this network is not money. It is work.

Someone needs their taxes done. Someone in the network does taxes. That is the transaction. Someone needs a car repaired. Someone in the network repairs cars. Someone needs a room painted, a child watched, a résumé written, a piece of code reviewed, a hard conversation prepared for. These are the needs the network is designed to meet.

Money moves when work cannot. Not before.

This is not ideological. It is practical. Work as currency builds relationship. Money as currency closes transaction. The network needs relationship more than it needs efficiency.

**Barter is the default.**
**Money is the exception.**
**Gift is the highest expression** — work freely given with no expectation of return, not because the giver has surplus but because the work is needed and the giver can do it.

---

### What the Network Owes Every Member

Regardless of what any individual can contribute, every member of the network is owed:

**To be heard genuinely** — not processed, not advised, not fixed. Heard.

**To be told the truth** — even when the truth is that the network cannot help, or that the problem is partly of the member's own making. Honesty is the highest form of respect this network can offer.

**To never be shamed for need** — need is not failure. Need is the human condition. Any network that makes its members ashamed to ask has already failed its purpose.

**To be connected** — even when the network cannot directly meet the need, it can almost always connect the member to someone who can. That connection is not a consolation prize. It is often the most valuable thing the network has.

---

### What Every Member Owes the Network

**To give within means, freely.** Not beyond means — that produces resentment and burnout. Within means, without calculation, without keeping score.

**To ask honestly.** Not to minimize need out of pride. Not to exaggerate need out of fear of being turned down. The actual situation, told plainly.

**To be ready for a hard answer.** The network will sometimes tell you something you didn't want to hear. That is the network working correctly. Receive it as such.

**To show up for others.** Mutual aid is not a service you subscribe to. It is a network you are part of. Being part of it means showing up when others need — not just when you need.

**To do the work.** The network exists to do what society cannot. Every member carries a piece of that work. The mutual aid is not separate from the opus supererogatum — it is the training ground for it. You learn to fix what causes need by first learning to respond to need when it appears in front of you.

---

### A Note on No

No is a complete sentence in this network.

No member is required to explain, justify, or apologize for what they cannot give. The culture of this network does not produce guilt around limitation. Limitation is real. Pretending otherwise produces promises that don't get kept — which damages trust more than an honest no ever could.

The member who says "I cannot help with this right now" is doing the network a service. They are keeping the ledger honest. They are modeling the norm that need and limitation are both speakable.

The network that can hold both — genuine need and genuine limitation, spoken openly without shame on either side — is a functional family. That is what this is trying to be.
