# CONTRIBUTING.md

## How This Works

Open Ritual 1.0 is licensed under **Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 (CC BY-SA 4.0)**.

In plain terms:

- **Use it** — run it, implement it, build with it
- **Fork it** — take the whole thing, adapt it to your context, publish your version
- **Modify it** — change what needs changing for your community, your culture, your moment
- **Share it** — distribute freely, in any medium, in any language
- **Credit it** — attribute the origin, link back to this repository
- **Keep it open** — any version you publish must carry the same license. You cannot close it, sell it exclusively, or make it proprietary. The commons stays common.

This license applies to everything in this repository except the initiation experience itself — which by design cannot be documented in a way that preserves its function. See The One Invariant below.

## Forking in Practice

Forking means copying this repository under your own account and adapting it for your context. Your fork is your version. It lives alongside this one — it doesn't replace it, and it doesn't require our approval.

Good reasons to fork:

- Your community has a specific cultural context that needs different language or structure
- You want to adapt the mutual aid protocol for a particular geography or need
- You're translating into another language
- You've learned something from running it that improves the framework
- The institution can't move — so you're moving without it

If your fork solves something well, consider submitting a Pull Request back to 1.0. We'll review it. We may incorporate it. We may not. Either way your work exists and is useful.

## The One Invariant

Every other element of this framework is forkable. The initiation script, the mutual aid mechanics, the progression structure, the language, the aesthetic, the meeting format — all of it can be modified, replaced, or discarded if your context demands it.

One thing cannot be forked away without breaking the mechanism:

**The candidate must never know what comes next.**

This is not a rule imposed from outside. It is the physics of how the initiation works. The confrontation with mortality must be genuine or it produces nothing. Genuine means unscripted from the candidate's perspective. Unscripted means unknown.

You can publish every word of your ritual publicly. That's fine — it's been done, it doesn't matter, the people who read it are not your candidates. What matters is that your candidate has not read it. The veil is not secrecy for its own sake. The veil is the medicine.

A fork that removes this invariant is not a broken rule. It is a broken tool. It will produce a workshop, not an initiation. That's a legitimate thing to run — it is not this thing.

## What Makes a Good Contribution

The best contributions come from running it and learning something.

If you implement Open Ritual 1.0 and discover that a particular element of the initiation framework doesn't land the way the documentation suggests — that's a contribution. Document what you changed and why. Submit it.

If your mutual aid network develops a mechanic that solves a problem the original framework didn't anticipate — that's a contribution. Write it up. Submit it.

If you run this in a context the original authors never imagined and it teaches you something — that's a contribution. Tell us what you learned.

The contribution we most want is the one that comes from the work actually being done in the world. Theory is the starting point. Practice is the test.

## What This Is Not For

This framework is not a product. It is not a brand. It is not a vehicle for building a centralized organization, a certification body, or a paid membership community.

Anyone can implement it. Nobody owns it. No one can grant or revoke legitimacy. There is no headquarters. There is no treasurer. There is no Grand Lodge of Open Ritual.

If you find yourself building those things around it — stop and ask whether you are serving the ideal or whether the institution is becoming the end in itself.

Lessing asked that question in 1778. It is still the right question.

## On AI-Assisted Development

This framework was developed through human-AI collaboration and is openly acknowledged as such.

The ideas, the intellectual lineage, the lived experience in the craft, the pedagogical practice, the diagnosis of what is broken and why — those belong to the human. The AI contributed synthesis, structure, and the ability to keep up with a fast-moving mind.

We believe AI-assisted work carries a particular obligation toward open source. The models were trained on the collective output of human civilization — every book, paper, forum post, and piece of code ever written. That is not any individual's labor in a clean sense. Keeping the outputs of that process proprietary is philosophically incoherent given what produced the tool.

The commons built this. The commons gets it back.
