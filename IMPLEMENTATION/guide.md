## How to Start

### The Minimum Viable Node

Twelve people. That is the only hard requirement.

Not twelve of a particular gender, class, religion, or background. Twelve people who have found this document, understood what it is asking, and are willing to do the work. The diversity of those twelve is not a nice-to-have — it is the mechanism. A node of twelve people who are all the same has already failed Lessing's test before it begins.

How you find them is your problem to solve. The document cannot solve it for you and shouldn't try. What it can tell you is what you are looking for: people who are drawn to the work, not the idea of the work. People who read this and felt something shift rather than people who thought it sounded interesting. The difference will become apparent quickly.

---

### Before Anyone Is Initiated

The founding twelve meet once before any initiation occurs. This is not an initiation. It is a recognition — twelve people in a room acknowledging that they have found each other and intend to build something.

This meeting has one agenda:

**Read the commitment aloud together.**

Not discussed. Not workshopped. Read. All twelve voices, the same words, at the same time. This is the founding act. It constitutes the group as a group before any individual has been initiated into it.

The commitment:

*I will do what this network exists to do. I will work on the problems society causes, not petition society to solve them. I am now obligated to the people in this room and to everyone who makes the same commitment. Their need is my concern. My need is their concern. I will bring what is true rather than what is convenient. I will say the hard thing. I will hear the hard thing.*

After this is read the group draws lots for initiation order. Chance determines who goes first. No hierarchy is implied or intended. The first person initiated carries no more authority than the last — they simply carry the obligation longer.

---

### The Initiation Sequence

The founding twelve initiate each other in the order determined by lot.

The first initiation is run by the remaining eleven collectively — no single facilitator, the group shares the function. This is harder and it is supposed to be harder. The founding group has no experienced facilitator. What it has is the document, the commitment, and each other. That is enough.

After the first initiation the person who has been through it joins the facilitating group. Each subsequent initiation is run by everyone who has already been initiated. The group grows its own capacity one person at a time.

By the twelfth initiation eleven people who have been through it are present for the one who hasn't. That final initiation is witnessed by the whole node. It is the completion of the founding sequence and should be treated as such.

---

### The Founding Completion

When all twelve have been initiated the node holds a second collective meeting. This is the moment the network becomes operational.

This meeting has one agenda:

**Draw lots for the twelve roles.**

The roles are not prescribed. Before this meeting the group identifies what functions it actually needs — who coordinates mutual aid requests, who keeps the records, who reaches out to members who have gone quiet, who manages the node's relationship with other nodes if they exist. The number of functions should be twelve. The names of those functions belong to the group.

Lots are drawn. Roles are assigned by chance. The person who draws a role they feel unqualified for is encountering the second degree in administrative form — the gap between what they know and what is being asked of them. That is not a problem. That is the work.

Roles are held for approximately one year. The year is a natural cycle — a death and a rebirth, a complete rotation. Holding a role for a year means watching it through all its seasons, understanding it fully, and then releasing it. The release is as important as the holding.

At the end of the year lots are drawn again. The same person may draw the same role. Chance decides. This is intentional.

### Monthly Rhythm

Nodes meet monthly. This is a recommendation not a requirement.

Monthly maps onto the lunar cycle — another natural rhythm that connects the work to something larger than the calendar. It is also practically sustainable. Weekly is too much for most people's lives. Quarterly is too infrequent to maintain genuine relationship.

Each monthly meeting has three components:

**Opening** — the commitment read aloud together. Every meeting, every time. This is not ritual for its own sake. It is a regular recalibration — twelve people reminding themselves and each other what they are here for.

**Mutual aid** — open dialogue about needs and offerings. Who needs something. Who has something to give. The conversation follows the protocol in `CORE/mutual-aid.md`. No agenda beyond what is actually present in the room.

**The work** — whatever the opus supererogatum looks like for this node right now. A project, a problem, a community need being addressed. This is what the node exists for. The mutual aid is the training ground. This is the point.

**Closing** — silence. Duration determined by the group. The length of the silence is itself a practice — sitting with what has been said and done without immediately moving to the next thing.

### Virtual Nodes

This framework runs fully virtually. A node whose twelve members are distributed across geography is not a lesser node — it may in some ways be a purer expression of what this is trying to do, operating across the divisions of place that civil society reproduces.

Virtual initiation requires additional care with environment — the facilitating group must work harder to create the conditions for genuine threshold experience without shared physical space. This is solvable. Darkness, sound, breath work, and temporal disruption all translate across a screen. Physical vulnerability requires more creativity but is not impossible.

Virtual mutual aid is in some ways more powerful than in-person — the network can move resources, skills, and connections across distances that would make in-person mutual aid impossible.

The one thing virtual cannot fully replicate is the embodied presence of reception after ordeal. Nodes should build in moments of genuine human contact — even if the primary meeting culture is virtual, periodic in-person gatherings are worth the logistical effort.

---

### Connecting Nodes

When a node has been operating long enough that its members want to extend the network, any initiated member can help found a new node. They bring the framework, their experience, and their obligation. They do not bring authority over the new node.

Nodes are sovereign. There is no hierarchy between them. A node founded by a member of another node owes that node nothing except the framework itself — which is open source and belongs to no one.

Nodes may choose to connect — sharing resources, mutual aid capacity, and knowledge across the network. This connection is voluntary and bilateral. No node can compel another.

The network grows the way the open source movement grows — not through central coordination but through replication of something that works. Each node that functions well is an advertisement for the framework. Each fork that improves something feeds back into the commons.

---

### When a Node Fails

Nodes will fail. This is not a problem to be solved — it is a feature of a decentralized system that takes no more energy to maintain than the members choose to give it.

A node that loses members below a functional threshold should not be artificially sustained. It should be closed with the same intentionality with which it was opened — a final meeting, the commitment read together one last time, the acknowledgment that what was built mattered even if it is ending.

Members of a closed node carry their initiation with them. They can found new nodes, join existing ones, or carry the work forward alone until they find others. The initiation is permanent. The node is temporary.

The Spawn Masons built knowing it would be destroyed. They blew it up themselves when it was complete. That is not failure. That is the work done honestly.
